From Contract to Cash: Navigating Revenue Recognition Under ASC 606 and Ind AS 115

Revenue recognition is the heartbeat of financial reporting. This article explains how ASC 606 and Ind AS 115 apply the same 5-step model and where practical differences can affect global businesses.

Are global revenue standards helping your business—or complicating your financial clarity?

Revenue recognition isn’t just accounting—it's the story your business tells from contract to cash. Clarity in ASC 606 and Ind AS 115 builds trust, transparency, and long-term value.

1. The Core Principle: Same Backbone, Different Nuances

At their heart, ASC 606 and Ind AS 115 share a common goal:

“Recognize revenue in a way that reflects the transfer of goods or services to customers, in an amount that represents the consideration the entity expects to receive.”

Both standards are built on a 5-step model, but operational interpretations, disclosure demands, and application guidance create practical differences.

2. The 5-Step Model: A Quick Recap

  1. Step 1: Identify the contract with a customer

    A contract must be enforceable and approved by both parties.

  2. Step 2: Identify the performance obligations

    Determine whether goods/services are distinct and separable.

  3. Step 3: Determine the transaction price

    Consider fixed amounts, variable consideration, and significant financing components.

  4. Step 4: Allocate the transaction price

    Allocate the transaction price based on relative standalone selling prices.

  5. Step 5: Recognize revenue

    Recognize revenue when (or as) performance obligations are satisfied.

Note: While the framework is identical across ASC 606 and Ind AS 115, the application leads to different reporting realities.

3. Key Differences Between ASC 606 and Ind AS 115

  • Contract Modifications

    ASC 606 provides more detailed application guidance and industry examples. Ind AS 115 mirrors IFRS 15 closely and includes fewer prescriptive examples, meaning more judgment in some scenarios.

  • Collectability Threshold

    ASC 606 uses a probable collectability threshold commonly interpreted under US GAAP as approximately 75–80% likelihood. Ind AS 115 follows IFRS meaning of “probable,” typically interpreted as greater than 50%.

  • Non-cash Consideration

    Both standards measure non-cash consideration at fair value, but ASC 606 contains more illustrative examples that can change judgments in practice.

  • Sales Taxes and Levies

    ASC 606 allows an accounting policy election for presentation of sales taxes on a net basis; Ind AS 115 requires principal vs agent assessment—practical outcomes can differ by industry.

  • Impairment of Contract Costs

    ASC 606 refers to ASC 340 guidance for evaluating impairment. Ind AS 115 follows IAS 36 (IFRS) impairment guidance — resulting in differences in thresholds and methodology.

  • Disclosures

    ASC 606 has extensive disclosure requirements (and SEC expectations for public companies). Ind AS 115 requires IFRS-aligned disclosures that are typically less granular, producing different compliance burdens.

4. Why These Differences Matter for Global Businesses

Even small divergences can create:

  • Variations in reported revenue timing and amounts
  • Complexities consolidating group-level results
  • Increased audit scrutiny
  • Challenges in investor communication

For multinational entities, managing these differences is essential to maintain consistency and avoid regulatory risk.

5. Contract to Cash: Practical Challenges & Best Practices

a. Contract Design & Review

Contracts frequently include variable consideration, price protection, renewal options, and milestone payments. Finance should work with legal and sales to ensure contract language supports compliant recognition.

b. Performance Obligation Analysis

Key questions:

  • Are the goods/services distinct?
  • Should obligations be bundled?
  • When is control transferred?

Mistakes here can lead to misstated revenue.

c. Systems & MIS Integration

Common struggles include manual tracking, inconsistent contract data, and ERP limitations. Investing in integrated MIS or revenue automation reduces errors and speeds reporting.

d. Documentation & Audit Trail

Both standards require robust documentation around:

  • Transaction price estimates
  • Variable consideration judgments
  • Stand-alone selling price determinations

e. Internal Controls

Essential controls include:

  • Contract approval workflows
  • Review of estimates and judgments
  • Periodic reassessment of obligations

6. Industry Spotlight: Where Issues Most Commonly Arise

  • Technology & SaaS: Bundled offerings (software + support + upgrades) create allocation challenges.
  • Real Estate & Construction: Significant financing components and long-term contracts affect timing of revenue.
  • Manufacturing: Variable consideration (rebates, discounts) impacts transaction price.
  • Telecommunications: Principal vs agent evaluation influences gross vs net presentation.

7. Aligning Global Teams: A Strategic Imperative

CFOs with cross-border operations should prioritize:

  • Cross-border training on both standards
  • Harmonized policies and central revenue recognition manuals
  • Periodic global reviews and reconciliations
  • Automation for repetitive contract analysis

This alignment improves consistency, reduces audit friction, and strengthens investor communication.

8. Conclusion: From Contract to Cash, Clarity is the Key

While ASC 606 and Ind AS 115 share the same conceptual DNA, interpretation, disclosure, and application differences can materially affect reporting. Navigating these standards requires coordination across contracts, processes, systems, and controls.

Mastering revenue recognition is not just compliance — it’s a strategic advantage that strengthens transparency, trust, and financial resilience.
